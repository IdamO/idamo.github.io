My First Internship

Prelude:

        It is the January of 2015, and I have just started my sophomore year of college. Mind you, I stepped into the semester without necessarily having my eyes set on getting an internship. To be fair, I had never actually written any legit code (maybe except for a bit of MATLAB), so I knew my chances of getting a "tech" related interview was well in the gutters. I was right...
        Skip ahead to February. It had been a full month since I began writing code. My lack of insight into the world of programming made me believe that event I had attained “Legend“ status. I was grasping the concepts a lot quicker than I thought, was even tutoring some of my classmates about basic algorithms I had discovered while playing around with code. I felt unstoppable! This confidence pushed me to apply for internships. After working on many small projects (mainly an image analysis and photo filter tool), I decided to apply to a handful of companies. That leads us to the next section.

The Goldman Sachs Interview

        After an expected wave of rejection letters, I woke up to an email from a Goldman Sachs recruiter saying that she wanted to set me up for an interview with someone from the Technology sector. WHAT?! A Wall Street interview after just a month of coding? I could not believe it. Here’s the catch though, she wanted me to do it the next day! With all the blatant rejections, I was not really ready for an interview the next day. Then again, I was not about to back down and let this slip.

First Round (Phone Interview):
        The interviewer was very punctual and after a brief intro from both parties, we went straight into the technical stuff. This interview was over the phone, so most of it was theoretical and logic based, that is, as opposed to actual code writing. Although my programming class was based on C, all the questions were Java-based (as it is a better match to the proprietary language of Goldman Sachs). I was asked some basic questions about Object Oriented Principles (OOP) and some Java-specific questions involving why I think String objects are immutable, JVM and how the computer understands Java code. OOP questions dealt mainly around inheritance, polymorphism, interfaces and class relationships. Some math questions were also thrown in here and there.
        All in all, the interviewer was really nice and patient. Some of the answers came to me longer than they should have, but he was fine with that. I guess he understood the pressure I was under, having once been an intern. At the end of the interview, he gave me an overview of his own internship experience, 3 years prior, and the impact it had on both him and the company as a whole.

TL;DR I got called and flown out for the final interviews at Jersey City, NJ.

Final Round (On-Site Interview):
        This consisted of 2 back to back 45-minute interviews.
